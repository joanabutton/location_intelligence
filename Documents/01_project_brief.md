# Project Brief

## Location Intelligence for Retail Site Selection

### Urban Opportunity Mapping for an Independent Neighbourhood Café in Porto

## 1. Project Purpose

This project aims to develop a data-driven location intelligence methodology for evaluating where a specific retail concept is most likely to succeed within Porto.

The pilot use case is an independent, family-oriented neighbourhood café. The café is not a chain, tourist café, grab-and-go coffee shop, or traditional Portuguese café. It is a community-focused, design-conscious space built around local regular customers and repeat visits.

The wider ambition is to create a reusable framework that can later be adapted to other business types, such as co-working spaces, clinics, childcare facilities, retail businesses, and family-oriented neighbourhood services.

## 2. Core Business Question

Which locations within Porto offer the strongest opportunity for establishing a successful independent, family-oriented neighbourhood café?

## 3. Project Logic

The project is based on one central principle:

A location is not simply “good” or “bad.”
A location is suitable only in relation to a specific business model.

Therefore, the analysis will not simply identify busy or central areas. It will evaluate urban locations according to how well they match the needs of this particular café concept.

## 4. Client Persona

The client is an independent entrepreneur who wants to open a neighbourhood café in Porto.

They are not trying to build a scalable chain or a tourist-facing brand. Their goal is to create a financially viable local business with a strong sense of place, repeat customers, and community presence.

The client values:

Local loyalty over tourist volume
Quality and atmosphere over speed
Neighbourhood identity over generic branding
Family friendliness without becoming a children’s venue
Design quality without becoming exclusive or cold
Sustainable daily demand rather than short-term novelty

The client needs a practical, evidence-based location recommendation that balances commercial viability with the character of the business.

## 5. Café Concept Persona

The café is an independent, family-owned, neighbourhood-oriented café.

It is designed for people who want to slow down, sit comfortably, read, meet a friend, bring a child, work quietly for a short period, or become a familiar regular.

The atmosphere is cosmopolitan but warm. It feels more like a carefully designed local living room than a commercial coffee counter.

The offer may include:

Specialty coffee
Flat whites and other less common coffee options in Porto
Fresh juices
Homemade cakes and pastries
Brownies, carrot cake, red velvet slices
Wholewheat vegetable muffins
Spinach-filled pastries and light savoury options
Healthy snacks
Books and magazines available for customers to read
A small children’s play corner
Comfortable, high-quality interiors
Beautiful but practical furniture
A calm, welcoming environment

The café depends on regular local customers rather than tourists. Its success relies on being embedded in everyday neighbourhood life.

## 6. Target Customer Persona

The ideal customer is a local resident or frequent neighbourhood user who values comfort, quality, and familiarity.

They may be:

A parent with young children
A freelancer or remote worker looking for a pleasant local place
A local resident who enjoys reading, coffee, and quiet social spaces
A grandparent or caregiver spending time with children
A couple or friend group meeting during the day
A regular customer who values being recognised by staff
A person with moderate-to-high purchasing power who is willing to pay slightly more for quality, comfort, and atmosphere

This customer is unlikely to choose the café only because it is cheap or fast. They choose it because it fits into their routine and feels worth returning to.

## 7. Initial Location Hypotheses

A suitable location is likely to have some combination of:

Residential population
Family presence
Schools, nurseries, or childcare nearby
Parks, gardens, playgrounds, or family-friendly public spaces
Walkable streets
Good public transport access
Moderate-to-high purchasing power
A visible but not overly chaotic street presence
Local identity and neighbourhood continuity
Nearby cultural or community amenities
Enough density to support daily trade
Limited dependence on seasonal tourism

A less suitable location may include:

Areas dominated by short-term tourism
Areas with little residential life
Office districts with mainly weekday lunchtime demand
Major traffic corridors hostile to lingering
Low-walkability environments
Places where people pass through but do not stay
Areas where the café offer would be too expensive for the local customer base
Areas already saturated with similar cafés

These are hypotheses, not conclusions. The project should test and refine them using data.

## 8. Analytical Objective

The objective is to create a suitability model that ranks areas or candidate locations in Porto according to their fit with the café concept.

The project will not predict exact revenue. Instead, it will produce an evidence-based opportunity score using urban, demographic, accessibility, and business-environment indicators.

## 9. Methodological Approach

The project will follow this broad workflow:

1. Define the business concept and customer profile
2. Translate the concept into measurable location requirements
3. Identify available data sources and proxy variables
4. Collect and clean geospatial and business data
5. Create spatial indicators for Porto neighbourhoods or grid cells
6. Analyse patterns and relationships between indicators
7. Build a weighted opportunity scoring framework
8. Map high, medium, and low suitability areas
9. Interpret the results from a business and urban-systems perspective
10. Produce a portfolio-ready case study and technical report

## 10. Possible Indicator Categories

The first version of the model should begin with a focused set of indicators.

Potential positive indicators:

- Residential density
- Family/children proxy indicators
- Proximity to schools and nurseries
- Proximity to parks and playgrounds
- Walkability and pedestrian accessibility
- Public transport accessibility
- Local purchasing power proxies
- Proximity to complementary amenities
- Neighbourhood identity or local-centre indicators
- Existing café and food-service density
- Street visibility and accessibility

Potential negative or mixed indicators:

- Tourist accommodation density
- Overconcentration of cafés
- Major road dominance
- Low residential population
- Low pedestrian comfort
- Office-only demand patterns
- Weak local community infrastructure

## 11. Expected Deliverables

The project should produce:

- A clear project brief
- A concept-to-variable framework
- A data acquisition pipeline
- A cleaned geospatial dataset
- An exploratory spatial analysis
- An opportunity scoring model
- Static and/or interactive maps
- A written technical report
- A GitHub repository
- A portfolio case study suitable for CV and LinkedIn use

## 12. Scope Boundaries

The project will focus on one business question and one pilot use case.

- It will not attempt to evaluate every possible café type.
- It will not attempt to build a full financial forecast.
- It will not claim to predict business success with certainty.
- It will not treat footfall as the only measure of opportunity.
- It will not use variables simply because they are available.

Each variable must be justified by its relevance to the café concept.

## 13. Main Risks

The main risk is scope creep. The project must remain focused on location suitability for one clearly defined café concept.

Other risks include:

- Limited access to direct footfall data
- Difficulty defining “success” without real café revenue data
- Overloading the model with too many indicators
- Using weak proxy variables without explaining their limitations
- Creating a visually attractive map without enough analytical depth

These risks will be managed by keeping the first model simple, transparent, and defensible.

## 14. Definition of Success

The project will be successful if it produces a clear, evidence-based methodology that can:

- Explain what makes a location suitable for this café concept
- Translate a qualitative business idea into measurable urban indicators
- Compare locations across Porto in a structured way
- Produce useful maps and rankings
- Communicate findings clearly to a business audience
- Demonstrate geospatial, analytical, statistical, and urban reasoning skills
- Serve as a strong portfolio piece for data, AI, and urban analytics roles

## 15. Immediate Next Step

The next step is to create a concept-to-variable framework.

This will translate each requirement of the café concept into:

- A measurable indicator
- A possible data source
- A spatial unit of analysis
- An expected relationship with suitability
- A confidence level or limitation

This framework will determine what data needs to be collected before any coding begins.
