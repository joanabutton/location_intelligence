Geospatial Location Intelligence · Porto

Where should a family café open in Porto?

A transparent location-suitability model that scores all 1,659 Porto census subsections by how well they fit one specific café concept — specialty coffee, a children's corner, a calm room, and a regular local clientele — then recommends where to start looking.

Darker = higher opportunity. The red star pins mark the top-15 candidate subsections — click one for its parish, score, and index breakdown. Zero-population subsections (river, port, parks, industrial land) are left unscored. Built with GeoPandas + Folium; base tiles © OpenStreetMap, © CARTO.

How the score is built

Seven census- and OpenStreetMap-derived indicators, standardised to 0–1 and combined into four thematic indices, then a single weighted score from 0–100:

Opportunity score = 0.35 × Demand        (population density)
                 + 0.25 × Family        (children, family households, school access)
                 + 0.25 × Urban Quality (walking distance to a large park)
                 + 0.15 × Affluence     (education %, foreign-national %)

The weights are an explicit, documented expert-judgement decision — and a sensitivity analysis shows how much they matter. The baseline already reflects the family-oriented brief: a family-focused weighting leaves the ranking almost unchanged (Spearman ρ = 0.97). A premium-focused weighting produces a meaningfully different shortlist (ρ = 0.79, with only 43% of the baseline top decile retained). This indicates that affluence is not already dominating the baseline score. It also shows that the recommended locations change when the business positioning changes, which is precisely what a concept-specific suitability model should reveal.

What it says

Two lead candidates

The highest-ranked subsection lies in Aldoar, near the city’s largest park, and is particularly strong for the family-oriented, linger-friendly concept. Cedofeita contains a second cluster of high-ranking subsections, offering a stronger fit for a more central, moderately premium positioning. Both are marked on the map above.

Current scope and next validation step

Competition and tourism are not included in this first iteration. The next validation step is to examine whether existing café clusters and visitor activity reinforce or challenge the current candidate areas.

This is a deliberate first iteration: a complete, defensible thin model rather than a broad half-finished one. Known limitations are stated plainly in the repository, each scoped as a concrete next step.

  • Method highlight: park access is a real network walking distance to manually verified park entrances — not a straight-line buffer that would credit access across rivers and walls.
  • Reproducible: every phase is an independently re-runnable notebook accumulating into one canonical geospatial layer keyed on the census-subsection ID.
  • Explore it deeper: the repository ships a QGIS project pointed at the full analytical layer — style any variable, filter candidate areas with expressions, and overlay your own data.
